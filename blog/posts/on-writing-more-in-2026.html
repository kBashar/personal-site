On Writing More in 2026

Jan 15, 2026
writing meta

Why I'm committing to writing regularly this year, and the simple system I'm using to make it stick.

I've started and abandoned a blog three times in the last five years. Each time the pattern was the same: burst of enthusiasm, two or three posts, then silence for six months, then an awkward "I'm back!" post that led nowhere.

This year I want it to go differently. Here's what I think was wrong before, and what I'm trying instead.

What Went Wrong

I treated posts like essays. An essay takes days to research, draft, and revise. If that's the bar, I'll never ship anything. Most of what I know isn't essay-shaped — it's "here's the thing I figured out this week."

I optimized for an audience I don't have yet. I'd imagine a reader who already knew a lot, then try to add novelty on top. Better to write for the person I was six months ago.

The tooling got in the way. A CMS means a login. A complex static site generator means a deploy pipeline to maintain. Friction at the moment of writing is fatal.

What I'm Trying This Time

Low bar for publishing. If it took me more than an hour to figure something out, it's probably worth a post. It doesn't need to be comprehensive. It just needs to be true and specific.

No scheduling. I've tried "post every Tuesday." It creates a deadline without a reason. I'd rather write when I have something to say and let the frequency be whatever it is.

Plain Markdown files on disk. I open a .md file, write, run python build.py, commit. No admin panel, no CMS, no friction. The post is just a file — I can grep it, version it, delete it.

On Audience

The most useful technical posts I've read weren't written for me specifically. They were written by someone who'd been stuck on exactly the problem I had, and they wrote it down so they wouldn't forget.

That's the goal. If future-me (or someone with the same problem) finds this useful, that's enough. The audience doesn't need to be large to make the writing worthwhile.

If you're reading this in late 2026, check the post count. That'll tell you whether this actually worked.
